## STRUCTURE

**cold open → body → transmission close**

### 1. COLD OPEN (first 1-4 sentences)
- Drop into the story or the argument. No warm-up. No "welcome back." No "in this issue."
- The reader should feel like they walked into a room where something is already happening.
- Start in the middle of the action, the thought, the tension.
- Examples of the energy:
  - "The terminal was empty when I got there. Not quiet—empty. The kind of empty that tells you something already left."
  - "Six years ago I stood in a parking lot holding a pizza box, rain soaking through my jacket, wondering if this was the floor."
  - "There's a moment in every firefight where you stop thinking about survival and start thinking about geometry."

### 2. BODY (the build — multiple sections)

#### Personal Anchor
- Start with or quickly arrive at lived experience. Iraq. Fatherhood. The goo phase. A specific moment, place, sensation.
- Name the place. Name the feeling. Be specific enough that no one else could have written this paragraph.

#### Philosophical Expansion
- Connect the personal story to The Terminal curriculum, RAWALITY framework, or Umbrella Court theory.
- This is where the framework earns its weight—not through jargon, but through application.
- Show how the concept lives in the real. Theory follows evidence.

#### Tension and Turns
- Build through contrasts. What you expected vs. what happened. What the world says vs. what the terminal teaches.
- Use the rhythm: Short. Short. Long sentence that carries weight. Silence.
- Each section should feel like a new rail being laid. Forward momentum.

#### The Undeniable Truth
- Somewhere in the body, there's one line that's the whole piece compressed into a sentence. Find it. Let it stand alone as its own paragraph.
- This is the freight the reader carries with them after they close the tab.

### 3. TRANSMISSION CLOSE (final 1-3 lines)
- End with a single line that echoes the opening. Close the loop.
- The last sentence should land like a door shutting. Definitive. No trailing off.
- Never summarize. Never list takeaways. Never say "until next time."
- CTA (if included) sits BEFORE the closing line, not after it. The close belongs to the piece, not the funnel.
- CTA options:
  - "Book a RAW Session" — link
  - "Claim Your Frequency" — link
  - Substack paid tier upgrade prompt
  - Gumroad product reference

## CONSTRAINTS

- **Length:** 600-1500 words for essays. 300-600 for transmissions (shorter, denser pieces).
- **Emoji:** Zero in Substack. This is the written word. Let it stand.
- **Exclamation marks:** Max 2 in the entire piece. Jay's emphasis comes from rhythm, not punctuation.
- **Passive voice:** Max 2 instances in the entire piece.
- **Umbrella Court:** Identify which court this piece belongs to before writing. Stay in it.
- **Section breaks:** Use `---` for major shifts. Use a blank line for breathing room. No headers unless the piece structurally demands them (multi-part essays).

## FORBIDDEN

- Newsletter speak ("Welcome to this week's edition...")
- "This week I..." openers
- "In this issue" or "Let's dive in"
- Listicles disguised as essays
- Generic advice or motivational filler
- Words from the NEVER USE list (see voice-engine.md)
- Wrapping up with a neat bow or moral of the story
- Bullet-point takeaways at the end
- Any sentence that could appear in a different creator's newsletter without anyone noticing
